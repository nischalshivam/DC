Tony Montana has been dead since 1983. He has never been more popular.
Walk into enough gyms, barbershops, and bedrooms and you keep seeing the same poster. A man in a white suit. A cigar. A gold pinky ring. Staring out like he swallowed the world whole. Rappers take his name — there are hit songs literally titled “Tony Montana.” A Camorra boss in Naples reportedly built himself an exact replica of Tony’s Miami mansion. Saddam Hussein’s regime named a money-laundering front company Montana Management, after Tony’s operation. There’s a story film teachers love to tell, about students who showed up in Scarface shirts — and when the teacher explained how the movie actually ends, that Tony murders his best friend, gets his beloved sister killed, drives away everyone who ever loved him, and dies face-down in a fountain, alone, like a dog — they listened to all of it. And they still wanted to be Tony Montana.
Scarface is a warning. And the warning became the dream. That is the most fascinating thing about this movie — and the reason it happened is not that audiences are stupid. It’s that the film is doing something far stranger, and far more clever, than it usually gets credit for. So let’s actually talk about what makes Tony Montana terrifying — because it isn’t the chainsaw, and it isn’t “say hello to my little friend.”
Most of the great movie villains scare you through control. The ones who really get under your skin are quiet. Precise. They hold eye contact a beat too long, smile at the wrong moment, make you feel the danger long before they ever raise their voice. Their power is that they are never out of control.
Here is what people get wrong about Tony — they assume he’s the opposite, a man with no control at all. He isn’t. For most of this film, Tony is frighteningly in control. He can dominate a room, a deal, a terrified man across a table with astonishing force. He manipulates his way through an immigration interrogation. He carries out a contract killing without his hands shaking. He reads rivals, negotiates with a Bolivian kingpin, and takes an entire empire off the man who hired him. The wheel is in his hands the whole way up.
His problem is the brakes. Tony can control everyone in the world except himself.
That is the real terror of the film. Not a man with no control — a man with total control over everything except the one thing eating him alive. Listen to what he wants, because he says it flat out. Not money. Not a house. He wants what’s coming to him — the world, and everything in it. That is not a goal. A goal has an edge, a finish line, a point where you arrive. Tony’s want has no edge. It’s an appetite. And the whole film is the slow horror of watching that appetite finally eat the strategist who thought he was steering it.
He starts with nothing. In 1980, around a hundred and twenty-five thousand Cubans arrive in Florida during the Mariel boatlift — most of them ordinary families chasing a better life. But Castro also used the moment to quietly empty some of his prisons, and that’s the detail the film reaches for to give Tony his origin. He comes off the boat a criminal with a green card he has to kill for, and a dishwashing job he can’t stand for more than a few scenes before he says it out loud: he is meant for bigger things.
The man who wrote that hunger knew it from the inside. Oliver Stone was deep in his own cocaine addiction when he took the job — he said he did all the research on coke, in and out of the country. To actually finish the script, he had to run from it. He moved to Paris, somewhere the drug wasn’t in every room, and wrote the whole thing stone-cold sober. The man who created the most famous cocaine movie ever made had to get clean an ocean away just to write it.
And early Tony is all calculation. The first real test is the scene people remember for the wrong reasons — a drug buy in a cheap apartment that turns into a Colombian taking a chainsaw to Tony’s friend while Tony is made to watch. It’s grotesque, and it tells you exactly who survives in this world. Not the strongest. Not the smartest. The one whose nerve doesn’t break. Tony survives on pure, cold control. To build that coiled, predatory body, Pacino trained with knife-fighters and the boxer Roberto Durán, who he said had “a certain lion in him.” That’s the caged hunger Tony carries in every frame — total control on the outside, something pacing back and forth on the inside.
Now here is what most people miss about why this movie became a fantasy instead of a warning. Scarface does not deliver its lesson soberly. It puts Tony’s rise under pounding synths — Giorgio Moroder’s score, “Push It to the Limit” blasting over a montage of money and power. It dresses him in white. It gives him the biggest rooms, a marble mansion, a literal pet tiger, neon Miami glowing behind him, and the sharpest, funniest lines in the entire film. It made his violence so operatic the ratings board first branded the movie X. The plot is telling you, the whole time, that this man is destroying himself. The surface of the movie is telling you how electrifying destruction can look.
And it lets Tony be the one honest voice in a room full of hypocrites. There’s a scene in an expensive restaurant — Tony, wrecked on his own product, surrounded by respectable people who look at him like something stuck to their shoe — and he stands up and says it. You need people like me, he tells them. You need me so you can point your finger and have somebody to call the bad guy. Say goodnight to the bad guy. For one speech, the liar says the room’s ugliest truth out loud — and the movie makes you love him for it. That is the trick. Scarface condemns Tony in its plot and glamorizes him in every frame around it. The warning and the fantasy were never two separate things. That isn’t a flaw in the film. It might be the reason the film never died.
And here’s the cruel joke buried under all that glamour — it buys him nothing. He gets the mansion, the wife, the empire, and he is completely alone inside it. Elvira drifts through his palace numb and bored. He can’t really talk to anyone. He has everything he came for and he is miserable in the middle of it, because an appetite can’t be satisfied, only fed — and a man who only knows how to feed it has no idea what to do once the table is finally full.
Which sets up the most important moment in the movie. I told you Tony has no stable code — that almost every line moves the second it stands between him and what he wants. Almost every line. But not quite all of them.
Sosa orders a hit. There’s a journalist who needs to disappear, and Tony is right there, in the car, his thumb on the bomb. Then the target walks out of a building — and he isn’t alone. His wife is with him. And then his children climb into the car. And Tony — the chainsaw survivor, the man who shot his own boss in the chest without blinking — will not push the button. He will not kill the kids. He turns on Sosa’s man and kills him instead.
Now, that refusal doesn’t doom Tony out of nowhere. By this point the empire is already rotting — the addiction, the paranoia, the tax problems, the relationships coming apart. He was never safe. But that one act turns Sosa from a partner into an executioner, and it hands the collapse an army. And sit with what the film is actually saying here, because it isn’t the comfortable lie that crime doesn’t pay. It’s saying something colder. Tony’s world eventually demands the destruction of whatever scrap of humanity he has left. The one decent thing in him — the single line he won’t cross — is the exact thing that world cannot allow to survive.
And then the appetite turns inward and starts eating the man who carried it. The clearest casualty is his sister, Gina. Tony’s love for her was never clean — it curdles into something he can’t admit and can’t control, a need to own her more than protect her, to keep every other man away from the one person he’s let himself care about. So when he discovers that his best friend, Manny — the one human being who has been loyal to him since the boat — has secretly married Gina, Tony doesn’t ask a single question. He shoots him in the street. He murders his only real friend over a marriage, and only learns the truth as Manny is dying in front of him. There it is, the whole tragedy in one act — the appetite has finally eaten the last person who loved him, and Tony pulled the trigger himself.
After that, there is nothing left but the mountain. Tony sits behind a literal hill of cocaine, paranoid, raging, alone in his marble fortress, his face buried in the very thing he sold to the whole country. And then the army Sosa promised arrives. Tony goes out the only way a pure appetite can — swinging, roaring, refusing right to the last second to believe there is anything in this world bigger than his own want. Say hello to my little friend. He soaks up an absurd, almost cartoonish amount of gunfire, looking for a moment like nothing alive could touch him — until one man steps up quietly behind him and fires a single shot into his back. And Tony Montana pitches off his own balcony into the fountain below.
And look at where he lands. Face-down in the water. Dead. Everything he built already turning to nothing. And directly above his body, lit up, a statue with an inscription on it. The World Is Yours. The same phrase that floated over the city earlier on a blimp, the promise that pulled him forward the entire film — now hanging over his corpse like a punchline. The man who wanted the world drowns in three feet of water, underneath a sign that swears the world belongs to him.
That is the joke the whole film was building toward — and it was a joke baked in from the very start, because this was a remake. Pacino only made it because he loved the original Scarface from 1932, Howard Hawks’ gangster picture built loosely on Al Capone, a film made under the censors as a flat-out cautionary tale, even subtitled The Shame of the Nation. The warning was in this story’s DNA for fifty years before Tony ever hit that fountain. And audiences still walked out wanting the white suit — not because they missed the warning, but because the movie made the warning and the fantasy impossible to pull apart.
So what actually makes Tony Montana terrifying? Not that he’s a monster you could never be. The opposite. He’s a man who controlled everything except the thing inside him — and that thing is the same thing the world keeps telling all of us to feed. The promise that somewhere out there is a number, a level, a next thing that will finally feel like enough. The raise. The bigger place. The win after which you’ll finally relax. Tony chased “enough” for two hours and never reached it, because the dream he swallowed doesn’t contain an “enough.” It only contains more. Few movie criminals are worn as openly as he is — his face hanging in gyms and bedrooms and shops as a badge of ambition — and every one of those posters is a man face-down in a fountain, framed and put on a wall.
The World Is Yours was never a promise. It was always the punchline.
